00 — Overview

What is Hirenetix?

Hirenetix is an AI-powered recruitment platform built for modern talent teams. It combines automated candidate screening, smart job matching, real-time analytics, and collaborative hiring workflows — all in one place.

My work focused on redesigning the end-to-end recruiter experience: from how they post a job and configure AI screening, to how hiring managers collaborate on decisions and candidates experience the process. This wasn't just a UI refresh — it was a full product rethink driven by user pain and market gaps.

"Hiring is one of the most human decisions a company makes. Our job was to make the AI feel like a trusted advisor — not a black box."

My north star framing at kickoff
01 — Problem

Hiring is
broken by design

We started with a fundamental question: why do companies still struggle to hire well, even with an ocean of applicants? After 3 weeks of discovery, the answer wasn't a single feature request — it was a system failure.

01

Recruiter Overload

Recruiters were spending 60–70% of their time on manual screening, leaving little time for the relationship-building that actually closes candidates.

02

Inconsistent Evaluation

Without structured criteria, two hiring managers reviewing the same candidate often reached completely different conclusions. Bias entered at every stage.

03

Poor Candidate Experience

Candidates were ghosted for weeks, received zero feedback, and had no visibility into where they stood — damaging employer brand quietly.

04

Siloed Collaboration

Hiring decisions lived in email threads, Slack messages, and spreadsheets — making it nearly impossible to align a cross-functional panel quickly.

02 — Process

How we
found our way

We ran a tight but structured design process across 9 months. I acted as the design lead — facilitating research, running workshops, and owning the design system end-to-end.

01
Weeks 1–3 · Discovery
Listening before designing
I led 18 user interviews across 3 user types — recruiters, hiring managers, and candidates. We ran a competitive teardown of 7 ATS tools and mapped the hiring journey from job creation to offer letter. One pattern was brutally clear: everyone felt like the system was working against them.
User Interviews Journey Mapping Competitive Analysis Stakeholder Workshops
02
Weeks 4–5 · Synthesis
Finding the real jobs to be done
We clustered ~280 observations using affinity mapping and landed on 5 core design challenges. I wrote clear "How Might We" statements for each and aligned the team on which to tackle first. The PM and I then prioritized against business viability — we couldn't boil the ocean.
Affinity Mapping HMW Framing Prioritization Matrix
03
Weeks 6–10 · Ideation
Sketching at the speed of thought
We ran 3 design sprints — one per major workflow: AI screening setup, collaborative evaluation, and candidate portal. I facilitated Crazy-8s and dot-voting sessions with the team. We deliberately included an engineer in every sprint to catch feasibility issues early and avoid rework.
Design Sprints Crazy-8s Lo-fi Wireframes Concept Testing
04
Weeks 11–22 · Design & Test
Prototype, test, repeat — without mercy
We went through 4 rounds of usability testing — each round with 6 participants. We tested on Figma prototypes with realistic data. Key decisions like AI match score presentation and the panel scorecard format were significantly revised after testing. My rule: if 3 people are confused, it's a design problem — not a user problem.
Figma Prototyping Usability Testing Moderated Sessions Iteration
05
Weeks 23–36 · Build & Launch
Staying in the room through delivery
I partnered closely with engineering during implementation — attending weekly build reviews, catching edge cases early, and maintaining a shared QA checklist. Post-launch, I embedded in customer success calls for the first month to watch real usage and capture feedback before the next cycle.
Dev Handoff QA Review Launch Support Post-launch Monitoring

Throughout the process, I maintained one non-negotiable: every major design decision needed to be backed by user evidence, not assumptions. This built a culture of justified design inside the team.

02b — Key Insights

What research
actually taught us

A few insights that fundamentally shaped how we designed the solution:

🧠

AI needs to explain itself

Recruiters trusted the AI match score only when they could see the reasoning. A number alone created anxiety. Transparency wasn't a nice-to-have — it was a trust prerequisite.

🔁

Hiring managers live in email

We couldn't pull hiring managers into a new tool cold. The platform had to meet them where they already were — with smart digest emails and one-click actions.

📍

Candidates want progress, not promises

Candidates didn't need elaborate communication — they needed a simple, honest status update. The moment we added a timeline tracker, NPS jumped significantly in concept testing.

⚡

Speed is a feature

Every second of friction between "I want to post a job" and "the job is live" increased drop-off. We cut the job creation flow from 14 steps to 6 — and adoption doubled in pilot.

03 — Solution

The product we
built and why

The solution wasn't one feature. It was a coherent hiring experience built around three product pillars — each solving a distinct user pain, each reinforcing the other.

Pillar 01

Intelligent Screening — with receipts

The AI scoring engine ranked candidates by role-fit, but we wrapped it in a transparent explanation layer. Every score showed the top 3 factors, derived from the recruiter's own job brief. Recruiters could tune weights in real-time — making the AI feel collaborative, not dictatorial. This increased score acceptance from 34% to 81% in testing.

Pillar 02

Structured Evaluation — built for panels

We designed a scoreboard system where each panelist independently scored candidates on a fixed rubric before seeing others' feedback. This eliminated groupthink and gave hiring managers confidence they were making evidence-based calls. Calibration sessions were embedded into the workflow, not bolted on as an afterthought.

Pillar 03

Candidate Portal — dignity in the process

We gave every candidate a simple, branded status tracker — showing their stage, estimated timeline, and what to expect next. No platitudes. No form letters. Just honest, human communication. We also enabled automated but personalised rejection messages with optional feedback — something candidates had never expected and consistently praised.

"The hardest design challenge wasn't the AI. It was designing trust between the human and the machine — at every touchpoint."

05 — Reflection

What I'd do
differently

No project lands perfectly. Looking back, there are things I'd approach differently — and things I'm glad we got right.

  • →

    I'd involve candidates in research sooner

    We spent the first 6 weeks almost exclusively focused on recruiters and hiring managers. The candidate perspective arrived late, which meant we redesigned the portal twice. Starting with all three user types in parallel would have saved us a full sprint.

  • →

    The AI explainability layer took too long to prioritize

    We initially treated the match score explanation as a "Phase 2" feature. Testing proved it was fundamental to trust. We course-corrected, but it cost us 3 weeks. Lesson: when you're designing AI, transparency is always core — never a bonus.

  • →

    I'm proud we kept the AI humble

    There was internal pressure to let the AI make hiring decisions, not just inform them. We pushed back hard and kept the human firmly in the decision seat. That call was critical — it's what made recruiters trust and adopt the product instead of feeling replaced by it.

  • →

    Design system investment paid off late, but it paid

    Building a proper component library in Figma felt slow in month 2. By month 6, it was our biggest velocity multiplier — engineers shipped screens in half the time, and design reviews took 20 minutes instead of 90.

  • →

    What I'm taking forward

    This project solidified something I've come to believe deeply: the best UX for AI products isn't about hiding the AI — it's about making the AI's reasoning visible. People don't fear automation. They fear being left out of the loop.

Hirenetix is what happens when you design the process, not just the product

Smarter hiring isn't about replacing human judgment. It's about giving human judgment the right context, at the right moment, without friction. That's the design challenge I'm most proud of solving.
